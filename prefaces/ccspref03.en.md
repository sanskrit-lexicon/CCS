# Preface

The plan to compile a shorter Sanskrit dictionary, which was at first to serve merely as a glossary to the second edition of Böhtlingk's Chrestomathy, had already been conceived by me a number of years ago, and indeed in agreement with the highly esteemed editor of that work himself, under whose own hands the layout of such an auxiliary book — originally planned by him (cf. the Preface to the Chrestomathy) — soon broadened into that extensive work which now, as a Sanskrit dictionary in shorter form, is approaching its early completion. At that time my undertaking met with various obstacles, until a few years ago, through the kind accommodation of the publisher, the possibility was offered to me of carrying it out on an even larger scale than I had at first intended. The present work, which in content and form rests entirely upon the St Petersburg dictionaries, can therefore now pursue a twofold aim: it seeks both to meet the needs of the beginning Sanskrit philologist and to put into the hands of the comparative linguist, in the most convenient manner, the material useful for his purposes. The former I sought to achieve by taking as my basis, besides the Chrestomathy, several other particularly important texts — namely, among the Vedic, the 70 hymns translated by Geldner and Kaegi, the twelve hymns of the Rigveda edited by Windisch, and the passages from the Çatapatha-Brâhmaṇa translated by Weber in the Indische Streifen; among the post-Vedic, especially Nala and the dramas of Kâlidâsa (with attention to the most important editions, in particular the Çakuntalâ edited by Pischel) — for all of which this glossary is to serve as a special dictionary. The latter I sought to achieve by including in it all attestable roots and primitive words of secured meaning, particularly those belonging to the older language, so that it offers not merely a more or less randomly arising selection from the word-treasure of Sanskrit, but seeks to present that treasure itself, at least in its basic elements, with a certain methodical completeness.
