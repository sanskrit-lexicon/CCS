## Page 03 — Preface, 1 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [ccs_Page_011_Image_0002.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/ccspref/ccspref03.html)</sub>

The plan to compile a shorter Sanskrit dictionary, which was at first to serve merely as a glossary to the second edition of Böhtlingk's Chrestomathy, had already been conceived by me a number of years ago, and indeed in agreement with the highly esteemed editor of that work himself, under whose own hands the layout of such an auxiliary book — originally planned by him (cf. the Preface to the Chrestomathy) — soon broadened into that extensive work which now, as a Sanskrit dictionary in shorter form, is approaching its early completion. At that time my undertaking met with various obstacles, until a few years ago, through the kind accommodation of the publisher, the possibility was offered to me of carrying it out on an even larger scale than I had at first intended. The present work, which in content and form rests entirely upon the St Petersburg dictionaries, can therefore now pursue a twofold aim: it seeks both to meet the needs of the beginning Sanskrit philologist and to put into the hands of the comparative linguist, in the most convenient manner, the material useful for his purposes. The former I sought to achieve by taking as my basis, besides the Chrestomathy, several other particularly important texts — namely, among the Vedic, the 70 hymns translated by Geldner and Kaegi, the twelve hymns of the Rigveda edited by Windisch, and the passages from the Çatapatha-Brâhmaṇa translated by Weber in the Indische Streifen; among the post-Vedic, especially Nala and the dramas of Kâlidâsa (with attention to the most important editions, in particular the Çakuntalâ edited by Pischel) — for all of which this glossary is to serve as a special dictionary. The latter I sought to achieve by including in it all attestable roots and primitive words of secured meaning, particularly those belonging to the older language, so that it offers not merely a more or less randomly arising selection from the word-treasure of Sanskrit, but seeks to present that treasure itself, at least in its basic elements, with a certain methodical completeness. While I may now, as far as the layout of the book on the whole is concerned, count on the agreement of my reviewers, and while no doubt will prevail in particular as to the need for such a Sanskrit dictionary — neither too large nor too small — I believe I must justify with a few words the course I have taken in several other matters about which opinions may rather differ. That I have given no etymologies will surely seem to many a deficiency. But since precisely in Sanskrit, in more than ninety cases out of a hundred, even for the beginner — provided he has mastered the vowel-series and the most important euphonic rules — the matter is perfectly transparent, while the remaining cases are partly left wholly unexplained and partly so disputed that any treatment of them, however brief, would have been impossible without reference to the views of the various scholars and to the relevant literature — which latter method would in turn have stood in complete contradiction to the otherwise thoroughly elementary character of the book: I therefore held it best to dispense altogether with such explanations and rather to use the space thus gained for cross-referencing difficult inflectional forms to the headword — a procedure by which, in my view, more labour is spared the pupil than by reducing simple words to their roots or compounds to their components. Perhaps here and there one will also miss a more definite explanation of the technical terms, or, in the case of proper names, the customary remarks of a mythological or literary-historical character. Here too I believed I might confine myself to what is most strictly necessary, in the opinion that where the texts themselves do not already supply the explanation of difficult expressions — as, for example, in the passage from the Kâvyâdarça or in the Vedântasâra — the aids to be presupposed in the reader's hands (I mean, for instance, the translations and commentaries by Weber, or Geldner and Kaegi, on the Vedic texts, and those by Fritze on the dramas) for the most part already supply the substance in such a way that one need not look it up in the lexicon, and in fact does not. Lengthy definitions are therefore avoided where possible, and an expression has rather been sought which, even if it still calls for closer explanation from outside, nevertheless meets the needs of translation, while the recourse of retaining the Sanskrit expression still always remains open. That I have refrained from the Latin plant-names found in most dictionaries no one will, I think, reproach me as a particular fault. Such things belong, of course, in a large thesaurus, but can in my view well be dispensed with in a handbook such as the present one.

beneath the syllable in question — a notation which, as is well known, Leopold von Schröder first introduced in his edition of the Maitrâyaṇî-Saṃhitâ. From the accent, which, however, is carried through consistently only at the headwords, not in citations and cross-references, it will thus also be possible to ascertain from the outset a certain age of the accented word, whereas the inverse conclusion from accentlessness is, for readily understandable reasons, by no means always justified. A star in front indicates that a word — or a word-meaning or construction — is handed down only by the lexicographers or grammarians but does not occur in practical usage; a star behind refers to the expressions, otherwise not attestable, translated from the Prâkrit out of the Châyâs of the dramas under consideration here; a star both in front and behind thus has the sense that a word is indeed handed down by the lexicographers, but in practice occurs only as a back-translation from the Prâkrit. The actual Prâkritisms — that is, those formations of Sanskrit which rest on Prâkritic sound-laws — have not been taken into account here. Self-evident word-categories are as a rule not given; likewise the regular nominative and the regular feminine formation have been left unmarked. Here â is always assumed as the normal feminine to a, so that only î is expressly indicated. Of verbal forms I have listed in full only the present stems, which in a sense belong to the signature or identification of the root; the remaining forms only in so far as they have developed a specific meaning, as is frequently the case with the so-called Participium Perfectum Passivi — here designated by *p.p.* — or in so far as they play a special role in the texts under consideration here. It seemed to me superfluous to give more in this field, since Whitney's roots and verbal forms are easily obtainable by anyone. Only the attested gender and the attested verbal form have been given; where a gender or a verbal form appears alongside others as an exception, I have placed it in parentheses. With the abbreviations *d. g. j. r. rh.* the dramatic, grammatical, juridical, ritual-religious, and rhetorical technical terms have often been highlighted for the sake of greater clarity. The sign ○ always refers to the headword or to a self-understood part of it; ○— and —○ thus mean, respectively, the headword at the beginning or at the end of a compound (whereby the combination of a verb with a preposition also counts as such). Italics have been used for what is only explanation, not translation; naturally the boundary here cannot everywhere be strictly drawn. A fairly frequent use has been made of the parenthesis. It is meant to serve not only to maintain continuity in the presentation by isolating particulars, but also to distinguish the rarer from the more frequent (see above); finally, in many cases, to provide the double possibility of a translation or explanation. Cases such as the following are clear without further ado: अनन्यमानस "thinking of no other(s)", or चतुर्धा "into four part(s)", where both translations lie equally near.
