From the accent, which, however, is carried through consistently only at the headwords, not in citations and cross-references, it will thus also be possible to ascertain from the outset a certain age of the accented word, whereas the inverse conclusion from accentlessness is, for readily understandable reasons, by no means always justified. A star in front indicates that a word — or a word-meaning or construction — is handed down only by the lexicographers or grammarians but does not occur in practical usage; a star behind refers to the expressions, otherwise not attestable, translated from the Prâkrit out of the Châyâs of the dramas under consideration here; a star both in front and behind thus has the sense that a word is indeed handed down by the lexicographers, but in practice occurs only as a back-translation from the Prâkrit. The actual Prâkritisms — that is, those formations of Sanskrit which rest on Prâkritic sound-laws — have not been taken into account here. Self-evident word-categories are as a rule not given; likewise the regular nominative and the regular feminine formation have been left unmarked. Here â is always assumed as the normal feminine to a, so that only î is expressly indicated. Of verbal forms I have listed in full only the present stems, which in a sense belong to the signature or identification of the root; the remaining forms only in so far as they have developed a specific meaning, as is frequently the case with the so-called Participium Perfectum Passivi — here designated by *p.p.* — or in so far as they play a special role in the texts under consideration here. It seemed to me superfluous to give more in this field, since Whitney's roots and verbal forms are easily obtainable by anyone. Only the attested gender and the attested verbal form have been given; where a gender or a verbal form appears alongside others as an exception, I have placed it in parentheses. With the abbreviations *d. g. j. r. rh.* the dramatic, grammatical, juridical, ritual-religious, and rhetorical technical terms have often been highlighted for the sake of greater clarity. The sign ○ always refers to the headword or to a self-understood part of it; ○— and —○ thus mean, respectively, the headword at the beginning or at the end of a compound (whereby the combination of a verb with a preposition also counts as such). Italics have been used for what is only explanation, not translation; naturally the boundary here cannot everywhere be strictly drawn. A fairly frequent use has been made of the parenthesis. It is meant to serve not only to maintain continuity in the presentation by isolating particulars, but also to distinguish the rarer from the more frequent (see above); finally, in many cases, to provide the double possibility of a translation or explanation. Cases such as the following are clear without further ado: अनन्यमानस "thinking of no other(s)", or चतुर्धा "into four part(s)", where both translations lie equally near. But otherwise too one will easily recognize what is proper and what is improper, as in इष्टि "a (simple) offering", स्वयुज् "one's own companion", and the like.
