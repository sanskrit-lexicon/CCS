While I may now, as far as the layout of the book on the whole is concerned, count on the agreement of my reviewers, and while no doubt will prevail in particular as to the need for such a Sanskrit dictionary — neither too large nor too small — I believe I must justify with a few words the course I have taken in several other matters about which opinions may rather differ. That I have given no etymologies will surely seem to many a deficiency. But since precisely in Sanskrit, in more than ninety cases out of a hundred, even for the beginner — provided he has mastered the vowel-series and the most important euphonic rules — the matter is perfectly transparent, while the remaining cases are partly left wholly unexplained and partly so disputed that any treatment of them, however brief, would have been impossible without reference to the views of the various scholars and to the relevant literature — which latter method would in turn have stood in complete contradiction to the otherwise thoroughly elementary character of the book: I therefore held it best to dispense altogether with such explanations and rather to use the space thus gained for cross-referencing difficult inflectional forms to the headword — a procedure by which, in my view, more labour is spared the pupil than by reducing simple words to their roots or compounds to their components. Perhaps here and there one will also miss a more definite explanation of the technical terms, or, in the case of proper names, the customary remarks of a mythological or literary-historical character. Here too I believed I might confine myself to what is most strictly necessary, in the opinion that where the texts themselves do not already supply the explanation of difficult expressions — as, for example, in the passage from the Kâvyâdarça or in the Vedântasâra — the aids to be presupposed in the reader's hands (I mean, for instance, the translations and commentaries by Weber, or Geldner and Kaegi, on the Vedic texts, and those by Fritze on the dramas) for the most part already supply the substance in such a way that one need not look it up in the lexicon, and in fact does not. Lengthy definitions are therefore avoided where possible, and an expression has rather been sought which, even if it still calls for closer explanation from outside, nevertheless meets the needs of translation, while the recourse of retaining the Sanskrit expression still always remains open. That I have refrained from the Latin plant-names found in most dictionaries no one will, I think, reproach me as a particular fault. Such things belong, of course, in a large thesaurus, but can in my view well be dispensed with in a handbook such as the present one.
